\section{Metrics, Signals, and Features}
\label{sec:metrics-signals-features}

Raw observations are not model variables. Metrics, spans, logs, runtime
profiles, queue events, worker lifecycle events, and domain events become useful
after they are mapped into semantic roles.

Let $s$ be a raw signal from an observation source. A feature mapping is a typed
transformation
\[
M_j:s\mapsto m_j,
\]
where $m_j$ is a derived model variable with an assigned influence domain. The
target may be a canonical input feature, an observed cost value, a symptom, an
outcome, diagnostic evidence, or risk evidence.

The mapping target matters. A packet-loss metric may become a
network-condition context feature. Worker reserve and release events may become
observed worker-slot occupancy. A dependency timeout log may become a symptom or
outcome. A sustained queue delay may become risk evidence.

Canonical features form the input vector $z_a$. Observed output values such as
$Y_q(a)$ are not part of $z_a$ for the same attempt; they are produced during or
after execution and used for residuals, calibration, diagnostics, and future
model updates.
